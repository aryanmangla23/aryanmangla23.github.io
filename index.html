Aryan Mangla

I’m a B.S. student in Neuroscience at The University of Texas at Austin, also pursuing certificates in Pre-Health Professions and Forensic Science. My research interests lie at the intersection of brain-computer interfaces (BCIs), neuromodulation, systems neuroscience, and neurorehabilitation. I’m passionate about behavioral and computational neuroscience, particularly advancing BCIs as both a research tool and a therapeutic technology. Long-term, I aim to pursue an MD–PhD with a specialization in translational neuroscience and neural engineering.

I currently conduct research in the Center for Perceptual Systems with Dr. Thomas Elston. The lab investigates how the prefrontal cortex supports cognitive control and value-based decision-making. Using behavioral tasks, multi-area Neuropixel recordings in non-human primates, and closed-loop intracranial stimulation, the lab aims to decode and modulate neural dynamics involved in control processes. This work lays the foundation for next-generation BCIs that may help treat disorders such as addiction and OCD. As the lab ramps up its non-human primate experiments, my role has focused on modeling human behavior to understand decision biases in probability perception and risk. I apply reinforcement learning (RL) models and EEG data to investigate how cognitive control modulates value-based choice. This work contributes to the lab’s broader mission to uncover neural signatures of value and decision-making.

At the Waggoner Center for Alcohol and Addiction Research, I am also a research assistant with Dr. Dayne Mayfield, where I studied SLC1A3 (GLAST) dysregulation in alcohol use disorder. I help conduct gene knockdown experiments in mice using AAV vectors, perform behavioral assays, and analyze molecular outcomes. Prior, I investigated at the College of Natural Sciences under Dr. Soo-Hyun Yang, the functional impact of cancer-associated mutations in the PARP1 gene to probe its role in DNA repair and disease, with implications for targeted therapies in both oncology and neurology.

I’m always excited to collaborate on projects—feel free to get in touch!
